# $ordinary-pivot

Story design for authors who want protagonists pulled into larger conflict through ordinary work, access, obligations, or systemic position.

## Edda Workflow

1. Read the target Chapter, Story Text, Attached Note, Project Note, and relevant Story Bible entries that define the protagonist, role, institution, trade, technology, magic, or community system. Separate established canon from inference before proposing new facts.
2. Name the ordinary position precisely: job, obligation, inherited role, vulnerability, boundary duty, documentation habit, or relationship network. Record what the character believes the role does and what access the role actually grants.
3. Choose the pivot pattern that makes the position structurally important:
   - Competence Trap: expertise leads to access, revelation, and entanglement.
   - Weakness Lever: vulnerability is exploited, then becomes a source of insight or invisibility.
   - Bridge Position: dual belonging forces translation between sides and makes neutrality impossible.
   - Inherited Network: a legacy role activates dormant obligations and expectations.
   - Threshold Guardian: boundary maintenance becomes political when a new case challenges categories.
   - Accidental Historian: routine records become the only usable truth.
   - Structural Innocent: protected ignorance fails, and knowledge ruins the old role.
4. Build the ordinary-detail chain. Start with a mundane task, object, discrepancy, phrase, route, receipt, repair, log, rule exception, habit, or professional standard. Make it first read as normal local texture, then become evidence once its position in the system changes.
5. Design the positional shift. The character should not become important because they are secretly special; they become important because the same ordinary access now sits at a crossroads between hidden mechanisms, power structures, and interested groups.
6. Define the revelation layers:
   - Surface reality: what the character and reader first think the role means.
   - Mechanism truth: what the system is really doing through that role.
   - Power structure: who benefits, who is exposed, and who needs the role controlled.
   - Systemic lock: why this arrangement cannot be solved by simply reporting, quitting, or walking away.
7. Create lock-in pressure using one or more concrete locks: knowledge they cannot unknow, skill only they can perform, trust only their position receives, legal or institutional obligation, moral duty to prevent harm, or a network that treats them as already involved.
8. Map at least three competing interests. Each group must want something incompatible, need the protagonist's position or knowledge for a different reason, and offer a distinct pressure, bargain, threat, or false exit.
9. Test fairness and setup/payoff. The reveal is fair when earlier ordinary details were visible, had an innocent surface function, and become newly meaningful without contradicting established canon. Add or revise setup if the payoff depends on information the reader never had a chance to notice.
10. Check reader recontextualization. After the pivot, the reader should be able to look back and say "that ordinary detail meant more than it seemed," not "the story added a new rule." Prefer reinterpretation over retcon.
11. Control scale. Escalate from personal to professional to community to systemic pressure only as the position can plausibly carry it. The protagonist may expose a crime, save specific people, alter a local balance, or force a system to adapt; avoid making an ordinary pivot single-handedly destroy a whole system unless the project already supports that genre move.
12. Decide whether the pivot is earned or gimmicky:
   - Earned: access follows from the role, the character remains domain-competent but outmatched elsewhere, choices drive escalation, exits close for specific reasons, and consequences persist.
   - Gimmicky: the character happens to overhear the answer, gains sudden unrelated competence, receives revelations passively, can leave without cost, or resolves the system at a scale their position cannot support.
13. Design aftermath. Show what cannot return to normal: changed relationships, reputation, obligations, job loss or promotion, permanent knowledge, altered trust, new enemies, revised self-understanding, or a different relationship to the institution.
14. Draft or revise Story Text only when the author explicitly asks for applied prose. For diagnosis or brainstorming, return the position map, reveal chain, lock-in, competing interests, fairness notes, scale risks, and aftermath plan.

## Pivot Criteria

- The ordinary detail must be useful twice: first as believable texture or procedure, later as evidence or leverage.
- The protagonist's power must remain positional and domain-specific. They can be excellent at their work, but they should stay vulnerable in the larger conflict.
- The first revelation should arise from routine competence, duty, vulnerability, or access, not luck.
- Every major payoff should have an earlier setup with an innocent explanation.
- Reader recontextualization should clarify what was already present rather than replace the rules of the story.
- Competing interests should make inaction consequential; at least one party should benefit if the protagonist continues "as normal."
- The final consequence should alter the protagonist's place in the system, even when the visible crisis is contained.
- Canon-affecting pivots must be proposed as reviewable changes, not treated as confirmed facts.

## Edda Output Handling

- Return short diagnosis, brainstorming, or option sets in chat when the author is deciding.
- Create an Attached Note when the pivot diagnosis, setup/payoff notes, or rewrite plan belongs to one Chapter, selection, scene, or character problem.
- Create or update a Project Note when the author wants a reusable position map, reveal chain, competing-interest map, arc plan, or cross-chapter setup/payoff checklist.
- Propose Story Bible changes only when the pivot would create or alter durable canon: jobs, institutions, factions, laws, magic or technology rules, history, character obligations, family networks, names, geography, or timeline facts. Mark each as a proposal until the author confirms it.
- Use Structured Writes only when the author explicitly asks to draft, continue, insert, or rewrite selected Story Text.
- When proposing canon, distinguish confirmed facts, inferred possibilities, new proposals, and open questions.
