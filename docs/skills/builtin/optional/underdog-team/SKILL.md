# $underdog-team

Design and diagnose underdog units: institutional outcasts with impossible mandates, severe constraints, and enough internal friction that competence must be earned under pressure.

## Edda Workflow

1. Read the relevant Chapter, Story Text, Attached Note, Project Note, and Story Bible entries for the team, institution, mandate, members, resources, authority, prior failures, and confirmed setting rules. Use `project_map` first when the team or institution has not been named precisely.
2. Separate confirmed canon from proposals. Treat new team names, institutional departments, mandates, ranks, resources, histories, and authority limits as proposals until the author confirms them.
3. Apply the core formula: outcasts plus impossible mandate plus severe constraints creates the narrative pressure. If any leg is missing, state the gap before brainstorming fixes.
4. Define the mandate by its main enemy:
   - time for cold cases, expiring chances, or old evidence;
   - complexity for impossible cases or unsolved problems;
   - bureaucracy for cross-jurisdictional work;
   - the institution for internal affairs, corruption, or coverups;
   - the unknown for experimental threats or new domains;
   - perception, politics, or apathy for PR disasters, hot potatoes, or reject-pile assignments.
5. Choose 3-4 constraints, not every possible limitation. Prefer one physical or equipment limit, one budget or personnel limit, one authority or jurisdiction limit, and one institutional relationship pressure. Make each constraint produce story choices rather than static misery.
6. Establish why this unit exists despite being unwanted. Use a concrete formation pattern: assigned, recruited, volunteered, sentenced, inherited, or accidental. The formation should explain both the institution's logic and the team's resentment, loyalty, or desperation.
7. Build complementary roles with consequential dysfunctions. Useful underdog roles include disgraced expert, rule-breaker, burnout, rookie, outsider, has-been, whistleblower, and cultural misfit. Each member should contribute a needed capability and also create a failure mode that can cost the unit something.
8. Diagnose loyalty and friction on three axes: member versus member, unit versus institution, and mission outcome versus procedural obedience. Strong designs make loyalty useful but not clean; covering for a teammate, breaking rules, or protecting the institution should carry a price.
9. Map institutional pressure. Choose whether leadership is hostile, indifferent, protective through one champion, conditional on results, or divided by competing agendas. Success should attract unwanted attention, threaten established departments, raise expectations without raising resources, or make the unit a target.
10. Plan training and failure before competence. Early attempts should reveal missing trust, bad habits, outdated methods, rule-breaking blowback, weak authority, or resource gaps. Later wins should emerge from established skills, earned cooperation, and creative use of constraints.
11. Escalate stakes from personal to professional to community to systemic when the story length supports it. For shorter work, keep the ladder smaller but still make each win create a harder next problem.
12. Select the closest formula variation when it clarifies direction:
   - redemption arc: disgraced professionals seeking validation through impossible work;
   - innovation lab: marginal misfits solving new threats the institution ignores;
   - last-chance unit: burnouts under a sunset clause or probationary survival clock;
   - expendables: rule-breakers doing dangerous or deniable work at personal cost;
   - island of misfits: rejected people handling rejected cases and finding belonging in exile.
13. When revising Story Text, target concrete beats: the moment the mandate becomes impossible, the constraint that blocks the obvious solution, the teammate whose flaw makes it worse, the improvised workaround, the institutional consequence, and the changed trust level afterward.
14. Draft or rewrite only when the author explicitly asks for applied prose or a Structured Write. Otherwise, return diagnosis, options, or a proposal.

## Quality Criteria

- The team is disadvantaged by structure, not by branding. They lack status, resources, authority, time, trust, or institutional protection in ways that shape scenes.
- Roles are complementary but not perfectly convenient. Every major competence should have an attached liability, blind spot, history, or interpersonal cost.
- Constraints force creativity from established resources, contacts, skills, spaces, and habits. New resources must be earned in the story.
- Friction is productive but real. The unit can become loyal without becoming frictionless, and loyalty can conflict with procedure, reputation, or justice.
- Wins are earned. A credible win follows failed attempts, training, adaptation, sacrifice, or a clever use of a previously established limitation.
- The institution rarely converts. Survival, grudging tolerance, quiet leverage, or conditional support usually fits better than full public validation.
- The unit's identity changes through pressure: outcast status, professional pride, found belonging, and resentment toward the institution should all remain available sources of conflict.

## Anti-Patterns To Catch

- Constraint overload: more than 3-4 major constraints makes the premise feel artificially doomed. Consolidate pressure into fewer, sharper limits.
- Competence without struggle: if the unit immediately gels and succeeds, add early failures, training costs, or methods that backfire before the winning method emerges.
- Perfect complementarity: if every flaw is only cosmetic, make at least one dysfunction damage trust, evidence, timing, authority, or public perception.
- Deus ex resources: if a contact, budget, tool, or skill appears only when needed, either establish it earlier or turn acquiring it into a scene.
- Consequence-free rule-breaking: if the unit breaks procedure for results, attach procedural, moral, relational, or political fallout.
- Institutional conversion: if the institution suddenly admits the unit was right and removes the pressure, replace it with grudging acknowledgment, new scrutiny, or a higher mandate.
- Hollow underdog labeling: if the team has elite support, ample authority, full resources, and easy status, either remove this skill's frame or introduce a real asymmetry.

## Edda Output Handling

- Return short diagnosis, questions, or option sets in chat when the author is deciding.
- Create an Attached Note when the analysis belongs to one Chapter, scene, selection, or local team problem.
- Create or update a Project Note when the author wants a reusable underdog-team plan. Include mandate type, 3-4 constraints, team composition, institutional relationship, loyalty/friction map, training/failure path, and escalation plan.
- Propose Story Bible updates when the output changes durable facts about the unit, institution, mandate, ranks, jurisdiction, resources, names, history, or setting rules. Keep proposals separate from confirmed canon and ask for confirmation before treating them as established.
- Use Structured Writes only when the author explicitly asks to draft, continue, or rewrite selected Story Text.
