# Cliche Avoidance Methodology

Use this reference when `$avoid-cliches` needs more than a quick diagnosis. Keep the author's story function intact; the goal is not random novelty, but a conscious move away from default expression.

## Core Principles

First ideas are usually available, not necessarily appropriate. Availability correlates with repeated exposure, so the first workable version of a trope, pressure, obstacle, reveal, or role often lands at statistical center.

A familiar element becomes tired when every major axis matches the default pattern. Changing one meaningful axis can make the element feel fresh while preserving its function.

The author's instinct about what the story needs is often correct. The first expression of that need is the part to question.

## Orthogonality Principle

A trope becomes cliche when form, knowledge, goal, and role all match the expected pattern.

| Axis | Question | Default Pattern | Orthogonal Move |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Form | What is it? | The expected element for this genre or function. | Keep or alter the form deliberately, not cosmetically. |
| Knowledge | What does it know? | It understands the central plot, secret, threat, or protagonist's importance. | It has limited, mistaken, local, or unrelated knowledge. |
| Goal | What does it want? | It wants to help, stop, tempt, teach, expose, or rescue the protagonist. | It wants something independent that happens to collide with the protagonist. |
| Role | What function does it serve? | It exists as obstacle, ally, mentor, clue, love interest, victim, or mirror for the protagonist. | It has its own story logic; the main plot intersects with that logic. |

Key test: does this element know what story it is in?

If yes, it is probably orbiting the protagonist. Fresh elements often think they are in a different story. Their behavior makes sense from their own frame and becomes useful because of collision, not obedience to the protagonist's arc.

## Eight-Step CTF Process

1. Enumerate cliches.
   List what everyone would suggest first. Include genre defaults, famous examples, and the version that requires no explanation.

2. Extract functions.
   Separate the job from the form. Identify plot requirement, character development, reader information, emotional experience, thematic pressure, and logistical access.

3. Generate alternatives per function.
   For each function, ask what else could achieve the same story job. Try another genre's solution, a different scale, a contrary tone, or a less obvious social/institutional mechanism.

4. Find unusual combinations.
   Combine elements that do not usually pair: genre collision, tone mismatch, scale contrast, mundane logistics inside high stakes, or intimate stakes inside a large system.

5. Invert perspective.
   View the situation through antagonists, bystanders, institutions, investigators, witnesses, family members, employees, rivals, or future historians. Ask what they would do for reasons unrelated to the protagonist.

6. Import from different domains.
   Borrow logic from law, medicine, science, logistics, religion, finance, performance, bureaucracy, sports strategy, historical events, fieldwork, or craft practice. Import reasoning, not trivia.

7. Test character specificity.
   Ask what this exact character would notice, misread, prioritize, fear, exploit, or refuse. If another character could be swapped in unchanged, the element is still too generic.

8. Trace downstream consequences.
   Follow what the alternative enables, blocks, reveals, complicates, or requires later. Freshness without consequence is decoration.

## Statistical Center And Edge

Statistical center is the cluster of the 3-5 most common expressions for a narrative need. Center choices communicate fast and work reliably, but they create reader fatigue, predictable trajectories, reduced specificity, and low fertility.

Statistical edge is less crowded territory that keeps the same functional and emotional vector. Aim for 60-80% familiarity: the choice needs some explanation, but should click quickly once established.

### Vector Method

1. Identify the initial instinct.
   Name the chosen element, the narrative function it serves, and the emotional effect it creates.

2. Map the functional core.
   Common cores include stakes, pressure, sympathy, complexity, expertise, access, time pressure, moral weight, information, intimacy, contrast, status, and vulnerability.

3. List center options.
   Identify the familiar versions of that function. Example: "cannot quit job" pressure often becomes sick family member, debt, blackmail, contract, or protecting someone.

4. Push toward edge.
   Use one or more pushes:
   - Adjacent substitution: related but less common participant or source.
   - Complication layering: add an unexpected dimension that changes the dynamic.
   - Ironic inversion: reverse the expected pressure while preserving the emotion.
   - Category jumping: move the same pressure into another domain.
   - Specificity injection: add a precise, story-linked detail that changes future behavior.

5. Confirm the emotional vector.
   The alternative must still produce the required pressure, stakes, sympathy, access, urgency, or moral weight. If the emotional effect is gone, choose a different direction rather than pushing farther.

## Fertility Evaluation

High-fertility alternatives:

- Generate multiple scene or subplot possibilities.
- Create natural conflict with other story elements.
- Raise questions that propel investigation or decision.
- Connect to several relationships or institutions.
- Can evolve as the story progresses.
- Create downstream consequences without constant authorial maintenance.

Low-fertility alternatives:

- Serve one function and disappear.
- Need repeated explanation to keep working.
- Do not connect to the main plot, character pressure, or world logic.
- Create only one kind of scene.
- Have an obvious single resolution.
- Are unusual but inert.

Use the "and then what?" test. A fertile edge option should suggest at least two or three future complications, scenes, revelations, or relationship changes.

## Workspace Format

For a Project Note or systematic pass, use this table:

| Functional Need | Statistical Center | Edge Options | Orthogonal Axis Moved | Fertility | Risks |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| What the story needs this element to do. | The common versions to avoid or choose consciously. | Fresh options that preserve the function. | Form, knowledge, goal, or role. | New scenes, conflicts, questions, or consequences. | Fit, clarity, genre, canon, or overload concerns. |

## Pitfalls

Inversion as innovation: the opposite of a cliche is often another cliche. Rotate onto another axis instead of merely reversing good/evil, success/failure, savior/villain, or expected/unexpected.

Novelty over function: an original choice that no longer creates the needed pressure, stakes, sympathy, or information solves the wrong problem.

Skipping enumeration: defaults stay invisible unless they are named. Always list the common versions before claiming an option is fresh.

Form-only changes: replacing "FBI" with "corporate security" changes little if the new group has the same knowledge, goal, and role.

Protagonist orbit preservation: the deepest default is a world where every element exists for the protagonist. Give elements independent logic and let collision create relevance.

Pushing too far: if the edge requires long explanation or breaks emotional recognition, it has left the useful vector.

Pushing everything: leave some center elements in place for clarity and reader rest. Differentiate the elements that carry the most story weight.

Adjacent substitution without dynamic change: parent to aunt, cop to detective, mentor to professor, or debt to rent may still be the same relationship pressure.

Fertility neglect: unusual but inert elements are less useful than slightly familiar elements that create new story.

## Quick Diagnostic Questions

- Could this element be swapped into five other stories unchanged?
- Does it require zero explanation?
- Have readers seen this exact situation many times?
- Does it solve only the immediate problem?
- Does it behave as if it exists for the protagonist?
- Which axis is default: form, knowledge, goal, or role?
- What function must remain untouched?
- What edge option keeps the same emotional vector?
- What downstream consequences does the edge create?
- What familiar element should remain stable so the story does not become overloaded?
